New Place Resources

  • Arpadda
    A town mentioned in Neo-Assyrian sources. It has been identified modern Tell Rifʿat, which is about 25 km north of Aleppo.
    Creators: Claudia Horst
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker, Jamie Novotny, Andrea Squitieri
  • Arumâ
    A town mentioned in Neo-Assyrian sources that has been identified as modern Ḥirbe Ruma (Khirbet ar-Rumah) and Biblical Rumah.

    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker, Jamie Novotny, Andrea Squitieri

    Contributors:
  • Bīt-Adad-erība
    A town mentioned in Neo-Assyrian sources that is identified with the modern site of Tell Baqqaq, a ruined settlement mound located ca. 10 km south of Gir-e pan that is now under water.
    Creators: Claudia Horst
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker, Jamie Novotny, Andrea Squitieri
  • Bur-mar'ina
    A town mentioned in Neo-Assyrian sources that is identified with brmrn in Aramaic texts and the modern Tell Shuyukh Fawqani.
    Creators: Claudia Horst
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker, Jamie Novotny, Andrea Squitieri
  • City Eleusinion at Athens
    The City Eleusinion was one of the most important sanctuaries in the religious life of ancient Athens. It was the urban “branch” of the sanctuary of Demeter and Kore at Eleusis in use during the Archaic and Classical periods.
    Creators: Denitsa Dzhigova
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker
  • Dabigu
    An unwalled city mentioned in Neo-Assyrian sources that is identified with Tell Dābiq.
    Creators: Claudia Horst
    Contributors: Jamie Novotny, Andrea Squitieri
  • Dermech basilica
    A Christian basilica of Byzantine Carthage.
    Creators: Jeffrey Becker
    Contributors:
  • Doulopolis
    A community within the territory of Colophon. Although there is some controversy as to its status, the name suggests that the community was founded by slaves (or other dissidents) who supported Aristonikos against Rome between 133 and 130/129 BCE. It seems to have been reabsorbed by Colophon at an undetermined point after 130/129. Its location is unknown today.
    Creators: Ryan M. Horne
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker
  • Roman amphitheater of Carthage
    A Roman amphitheater built at Carthage.
    Creators: Jeffrey Becker
    Contributors: Ryan M. Horne
  • Roman circus of Carthage
    The circus of Carthage could accommodate ca. 45,000 spectators and may date to the third century A.D.
    Creators: Jeffrey Becker
    Contributors:
  • Sendim Roman villa
    A Late Roman villa site dating to the fourth century A.D.
    Creators: Jeffrey Becker
    Contributors:
  • So-called Palace of the Dux
    A large building complex located on the western hill of Apollonia, probably built between the fifth and sixth centuries A.D. Its position and size, the presence of a chapel with a reliquary, and some public rooms led Goodchild to identify it as the residence of the Dux.
    Creators: Valeria Vitale
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker
  • So-called West Church in Apollonia
    A large architectural complex that includes a three-aisled church with a projecting western apse, a small atrium, a baptistery, and other structures built up against the inner face of the western stretch of the city wall of Apollonia.
    Creators: Valeria Vitale
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker
  • So-called West Gate of Apollonia
    An unusual structure close to the water edge, on the western side of Apollonia, refurbished in Byzantine times. The presence of Maltese crosses led to its interpretation as a martyrion, but an administrative role has also been suggested.
    Creators: Valeria Vitale
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker, Tom Elliott
  • Theatre of Apollonia
    The theatre of the ancient city of Apollonia, situated just outside the city wall, on the east side. The construction appears to be late Hellenistic, with half of the seats cut out of the rock on the elevation of the acropolis, and the other half built up.
    Creators: Valeria Vitale
    Contributors: Jeffrey Becker
  • Tophet of Carthage
    The Tophet of Carthage, also known as tophet de Salammbô, is a hybrid sanctuary-necropolis of Punic Carthage.
    Creators: Jeffrey Becker
    Contributors: Ryan M. Horne

    Velitrae (modern Velletri) was an ancient Volscian settlement. In the Roman Republican period, Velitrae was attacked by Marcus Furius Camillus (379 B.C.) and again by Cincinnatus (377 B.C.); the Romans controlled the site by 365 B.C. The gens Octavia hailed from the city and a youthful Octavian spent time there.
